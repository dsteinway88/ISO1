Internet Safety Tips

  • Use only firm-approved software and browsers.
  • Exercise caution when encountering dialog boxes and pop-ups when browsing the Internet.
  • Stay up-to-date with social media privacy settings.
  • Only download programs in compliance with firm policies and guidelines.
  • Contact Information Security regarding suspicious dialog boxes related to running or installing software.

Common phishing scams

  • Fake electronic greeting cards
  • Emails that appear to be from a financial institution asking you to click a link to confirm you username and password
  • Emails that capitalize on a disaster or cause and ask you to click a link and enter credit card information to make a donation

Internet Access and Social Media

Most of us use the Internet on a daily basis and access social sharing sites such as LinkedIn, Facebook, and Twitter. These sites and your pattern of Internet browsing, can present security risks for the Firm. Software such as Adobe Reader, Flash Player, Java, and Internet browsers are often exploited by hackers to install malware or spyware on computers.

“Social engineering” involves sending an email or text to someone in order to scam the recipient, usually by inviting them to click a link in the message which will download malicious software. Passwords can be easily hacked when weak answers are given to security questions. Using information posted on a social media or web site through a process called “scraping” your accounts can be easily breached.

The Internet opens the firm to many vulnerabilities and it is important to not only be aware of the risks but seek to minimize them by the following best practices.

Phishing

Phishing is the name given to an email or internet scam that tries to “hook” you into sharing personal information. Phishing typically involves an email that appears to have been sent from a legitimate sender or organization. The email usually states that your information needs to be validated or confirmed, and prompts you to click a link and then enter your username and password. One of the most challenging aspects of phishing is that the scammers often “spoof” messages so they appear to come from someone you know, or a known organization. Because of this, it’s important to proceed with caution when you receive any email requesting personal information – even if it appears to be from someone you know.

The bottom line is that if you receive an email asking you to provide personal information, it’s best to open your browser and manually type in the address of the company’s main web site rather than clicking any links included in the email message. And don’t forget: sometimes it’s a good idea to pick up the phone and place a call to confirm that a request is legitimate.
